Description

During 2024, I served as an Adjunct Assistant Professor at Webster University's San Antonio Metropolitan campus, teaching graduate-level courses in Information Technology Management. Over three consecutive terms, I instructed the following courses across a combined total of 80 graduate students.

Courses Taught

ITM 5400 – Systems Analysis, Design, and Implementation

Spring 1, 2024 (29 students)  ·  Summer 2024 (28 students)

This course covers the full information systems life cycle, from initial feasibility analysis through structured design, prototyping, procurement, and implementation. Students examine the roles and responsibilities of personnel involved in systems development, as well as the communication and documentation tools that support each phase of the life cycle. I delivered this course in person across two terms, working directly with students in a classroom environment.

ITM 6000 – Final Project in Information Technology Management

Spring 2, 2024 (23 students)

This capstone course is designed for students who have completed all other required courses in the IT Management graduate curriculum. It requires students to synthesize, integrate, and apply the full body of technical and management knowledge acquired throughout their program. I guided each student through the development of a substantive final project demonstrating graduate-level competency in IT management practice.

Verification: Official faculty teaching appointment confirmation emails from Webster University (appointments@webster.edu) are on file for each of the three terms listed above.
Reflection

Teaching at Webster University was a uniquely formative experience, shaped in large part by the makeup of the student population I served. The overwhelming majority of students in my courses were international graduate students, primarily from India, many of whom were navigating both a rigorous academic program and the challenges of building professional lives in a new country. Teaching these courses in person created an environment of genuine engagement — there was real energy in the room, and the face-to-face format allowed for the kind of dynamic discussion and immediate feedback that is difficult to replicate online.

This cross-cultural dimension added meaningful depth to the classroom experience. Students brought perspectives shaped by different educational systems, professional norms, and relationships with technology — perspectives that enriched discussion around systems design decisions and organizational change in ways that a more homogeneous class rarely achieves. My DBA studies have deepened my understanding of how culture, structure, and technology interact within organizations, and I found that these conversations became richer precisely because the room held so many different lived experiences.

In ITM 5400, I connected systems analysis concepts to broader themes of technology adoption and organizational disruption — topics directly relevant to students who planned to work in IT management roles in industries undergoing rapid digital transformation. For many of my students, the frameworks we discussed were not abstract; they were preparing to enter professional environments where these decisions carry significant organizational and economic consequences. ITM 6000 was particularly meaningful. Guiding students through a capstone project demands more than content delivery — it requires mentorship. My own experience as a doctoral candidate gave me credibility and empathy in that role, and I believe that showed in the quality of work my students produced.

BUSI-2305 – Business Statistics

Fall 2024 · Aug 26 – Dec 14, 2024 · Tuesday & Thursday, 5:30–6:45 PM · Online Synchronous (Zoom) · CRN: 56111

This course covers descriptive and inferential statistical techniques for business and economic decision-making, including probability, sampling distributions, hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and linear regression. Students applied statistical software — including Microsoft Excel — throughout the semester to analyze real data sets. The course is part of the Business Field of Study and serves students pursuing degrees in Business Management and Operations Management.

Verification: The official course syllabus for BUSI-2305-PAC 015 (CRN: 56111), issued through the Alamo Colleges District / Palo Alto College, confirms my role as instructor of record and is available as a supporting document.
Reflection

Teaching Business Statistics at Palo Alto College offered a strikingly different — and equally rewarding — experience from my Webster courses. The student population was a genuine cross-section of the San Antonio community: recent high school graduates taking their first college-level quantitative course alongside non-traditional students who had been away from formal education for years, and working adults managing jobs, children, and coursework simultaneously. That range of backgrounds and life circumstances made every class session an exercise in meeting students where they are.

The online synchronous format added another layer of complexity. Holding student attention and fostering real engagement through a Zoom session at 5:30 in the evening — when many students were coming straight from work or managing household responsibilities — demands intentionality. I designed the course so that every statistical concept was immediately grounded in a business decision context, because abstract formulas alone will not hold a room full of students with competing demands on their time and attention. When students could see that hypothesis testing was a tool for making better hiring decisions, or that regression analysis could help a small business owner understand what drives revenue, the material became relevant in a way that pure mathematical instruction rarely achieves.

My doctoral training in research methodology and quantitative reasoning gave me a depth of subject matter knowledge I could draw on to answer the harder questions in the room — the ones that go beyond the textbook. More importantly, it gave me the ability to distinguish between what students need to understand conceptually and what they need to execute practically, which is not always the same thing. Serving this community reinforced for me the social value of making rigorous analytical thinking genuinely accessible to students who might not otherwise encounter it in a meaningful way — and who stand to benefit enormously when they do.
